Checking to see the difference in temperature internally vs. at the surface. Positioned the pyrometer so it focuses close to the center of the non-through hole that I machined. The crucible is right-side up (this is one with not a very tight fit, the first that I machined at the machine shop), and there are non-through holes on either side. The top and bottom surfaces inside the hole are ~0.1" and ~0.05" thick, respectively (if I remember correctly). This is a high-vac run with the turbo pump and the vent valve attached but in the closed configuration. After a few hours, the lowest pressure was ~3E-4 torr, meaning there seems to be a significant leak due to the vent valve (or because of how much bigger this crucible is relative to the one I've been using). Doing the next test with a blank attached rather than the vent valve will be informative.

There is thermal chalk inside the crucible (above alumina plate, between alumina plates, and below alumina plate) designed to melt at 1204 deg C.

Powder at all three locations melted, indicating that the internal temperature was much higher than the temperature at the surface of the crucible lid, even with the non-through hole. It seemed like the temperature of the powder increased the further away from the crucible lid it was (see picture).

This indicates there is at least a ~90 deg C difference between the pyrometer reading and the surface. Perhaps I could have an open crucible, and measure the temperature of the sample directly? Or I could have a hole in the crucible top. There will still likely be a fair amount of thermal evaporation if I do so.
